# The Devotees of Shiva: Markandeya and Kannappa

## Summary

When the boy Markandeya refused to die, Shiva granted him immortality. When the hunter Kannappa offered his legs as a throne, Shiva blessed him with eternal devotion. These tales show how Shiva rewards those who seek him with pure hearts.

## Full Narrative

In a distant village where the rivers sang hymns to the gods, there lived a boy named Markandeya. His mother, a humble weaver, would spin threads of devotion into her cloth, whispering prayers to Shiva. 'One day, my son will be a great devotee,' she would say, her eyes gleaming with faith. But fate had other plans. One day, the god of death, Yama, came to Markandeya's door, his chariot wheels creaking like old bones. 'Your time is up,' Yama declared, his voice like thunder. But Markandeya, clutching a tiny idol of Shiva, cried, 'I will not die until I have seen the Lord face to face!'

Yama, surprised by the boy's courage, raised his staff to strike. But suddenly, a whirlwind of smoke and fire surrounded them. Shiva appeared, his body adorned with ashes, his third eye blazing. 'Why do you dare to harm my devotee?' he roared, his voice shaking the heavens. Yama, trembling, fell to his knees. Shiva, with a wave of his hand, granted Markandeya immortality. 'You will live forever, but you must wander the earth, teaching others about devotion,' Shiva said. And so, Markandeya became a wandering sage, his story a beacon of faith for all who heard it.

In another part of the world, a hunter named Kannappa lived in the dense forests of the Himalayas. Every day, he would climb the mountains, not to hunt, but to worship Shiva. He would carve a small lingam from stone and offer it fruits and flowers. One day, as he knelt in prayer, a tiger attacked him. Instead of fleeing, Kannappa offered his legs to the tiger, saying, 'Eat me, but do not harm the lingam!' The tiger, moved by his devotion, left him unharmed. Shiva, watching from afar, was pleased. 'This hunter's love for me is greater than his fear of death,' Shiva said. He appeared before Kannappa, granting him the boon of eternal devotion. From that day, Kannappa's legs, though scarred, became a symbol of unwavering faith, and he lived to see the rise and fall of countless kingdoms.

Years passed, and the tales of Markandeya and Kannappa spread like wildfire. Children would gather around fires, listening to how Shiva rewards those who seek him with pure hearts. 'Even the smallest act of devotion,' the elders would say, 'can open the gates of heaven.' And so, the stories of these two devotees became a reminder that faith, like the river, never stops flowing—no matter how great the obstacles.

## Variant — North Indian Tradition

**The Hunter's Offering**

In this version, Kannappa is a blind hunter who, despite his blindness, worships Shiva with such devotion that the god himself appears to him. Shiva grants him the boon of sight, not only for his eyes but for his heart, allowing him to see the divine in all things. This tale emphasizes the idea that true devotion transcends physical limitations and is preserved in the 'Skanda Purana' as a story of spiritual vision.
