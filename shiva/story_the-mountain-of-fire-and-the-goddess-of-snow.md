# The Mountain of Fire and the Goddess of Snow

## Summary

When the fierce ascetic Shiva lived alone on Mount Kailash, a young girl named Parvati climbed the mountain to win his heart. Their journey from strangers to eternal partners began with a test of courage, patience, and divine love.

## Full Narrative

High in the snow-capped peaks of the Himalayas, where the wind howled like a thousand ghosts and the stars burned brighter than any earthly flame, lived Lord Shiva. His hair was a wild tangle of black locks, his skin the color of stormy skies, and his third eye burned with the power of a thousand suns. He meditated for eons, untouched by the world, his body adorned only with ashes and a tiger skin. But the mountain had a secret: deep within its icy heart lived a girl named Parvati, daughter of the mountain king Himavat and the snow goddess Menaka. She had grown up listening to the stories of Shiva, the great ascetic who had no equal in power or wisdom. One day, as she gazed at the moonlight reflecting on the frozen lake below, she whispered, 'I will marry Shiva, even if it takes my entire life.' Her parents laughed, 'He is a god, child. How can a mortal girl ever win his heart?' But Parvati's eyes burned with determination. She wrapped herself in a cloak of snowflakes and set off toward Mount Kailash, her feet crunching against the frost-covered stones. As she climbed, the mountain tested her. A storm of wind tried to blow her back, but she stood firm. A river of ice threatened to swallow her, but she crossed it with steady steps. When she finally reached the summit, she found Shiva sitting in deep meditation, his body still as a mountain, his breath like the whisper of the wind. 'Why have you come here, child?' Shiva asked, his voice like thunder. 'I wish to be your wife,' Parvati replied, her voice trembling but resolute. Shiva laughed, a sound like the rumble of distant thunder. 'You think a mortal can be my wife? I have no need for love or marriage. I am the destroyer, the ascetic, the one who dances in the flames of destruction.' 'Then let me prove my worth,' Parvati said. 'Let me be your wife, and I will show you what love can do.' Shiva's third eye flared, and for a moment, the mountain trembled. 'Very well,' he said. 'But if you fail, you will be cast down into the deepest chasm of the earth.' Parvati knelt before him, her heart full of courage. 'I will not fail.' For days, she served him, cleaning his ashes, lighting his fire, and singing hymns to the gods. She learned his secrets, his stories, and his pain. She saw the loneliness in his eyes, the weight of the universe on his shoulders. One night, as the moon hung low over the mountains, Shiva looked at her and said, 'You have passed my test. You are worthy.' And with that, he took her hand, and the mountain itself seemed to sing with joy. From that day on, Parvati became his wife, and together they ruled the mountain, their love a beacon for all who sought to find their own path. The lesson was clear: love and determination can overcome any obstacle, even the might of a god. And so, Shiva and Parvati's story became one of the most beloved in the world, a tale of how two souls, one mortal and one divine, found their way to each other through courage and devotion.

## Variant — Kashmiri Shaivism

**The Dance of the Two Flames**

In this version, Parvati is not a mortal girl but a manifestation of Shiva's own energy, the goddess Shakti. When she approaches him, she is not seeking marriage but trying to awaken his dormant power. Their union is not a romantic tale but a cosmic event where Shiva and Shakti merge to create the universe. This story is found in the Kashmiri Shaiva texts, where the divine couple represents the union of consciousness and energy.

_Source: Tantric Shaiva texts, particularly the Spanda Karika_
